Double jump to onboard - how game designers leverage mental flow to onboard players

Double jump is a common game mechanic that is often used in platformer games to allow players to jump twice in quick succession while in the air. This ability is particularly useful when navigating tricky or hazardous terrain, allowing players to make longer jumps, change direction mid-air, or avoid obstacles.

When it comes to onboarding new players, the double jump mechanic can be a powerful tool for several reasons. First, it provides players with an early taste of mastery. By learning how to time and execute a double jump, players can quickly gain a sense of control and confidence in the game's movement mechanics. This can be a crucial factor in keeping players engaged and motivated to continue playing.

Second, the double jump mechanic is relatively simple and intuitive, making it an ideal introduction to more complex movement mechanics that players will encounter later on in the game. By mastering the double jump, players can begin to experiment with other techniques like wall jumping, dash moves, or gliding, all of which can add a new level of depth and complexity to the gameplay experience.

Finally, the double jump can be a fun and satisfying mechanic in its own right, providing players with a sense of freedom and fluidity as they navigate through the game world. Whether it's leaping across chasms, dodging obstacles, or simply exploring the environment, the double jump can add a sense of joy and playfulness to the game that can help to hook players in and keep them coming back for more.

In many platformer games, designers will include a high wall or platform early on in the game that cannot be reached by a normal jump. This is often done intentionally to teach players about the double jump mechanic. By presenting players with an obstacle that cannot be overcome using their existing skills, designers can encourage them to experiment and try new things. Players may try jumping multiple times in quick succession, or try different button combinations to see if there is a way to reach the higher platform.

Once players discover the double jump, they are often rewarded with the satisfaction of overcoming an obstacle that initially seemed insurmountable. This can be a powerful motivator to continue playing, as players are now aware of the potential for mastery and discovery that lies ahead.

In this way, the double jump mechanic can serve as a key component of the onboarding process, helping to guide players through the early stages of the game and teaching them the skills they will need to succeed later on. By presenting players with a challenge that requires them to use this mechanic, designers can ensure that players understand its importance and have a chance to practice and refine their skills before moving on to more difficult challenges.

While the double jump mechanic is most commonly associated with video games, its underlying principles can be applied to onboarding users to other types of software as well. In software development, onboarding refers to the process of guiding new users through the features and functionality of a new application, software or website. One way to apply the double jump concept to software onboarding is to create a step-by-step tutorial that introduces users to new features gradually. By presenting users with a series of small challenges that build on their existing knowledge, designers can help to instill a sense of mastery and control that can be critical for keeping users engaged and motivated.

Just as a game designer might present players with a high wall that requires a double jump to overcome, software designers can create obstacles or challenges that require users to learn and use new features in order to proceed. For example, a user might be prompted to perform a specific action, such as creating a new project or adding a new user to a team, that requires them to navigate a series of menus or dialog boxes. By presenting these challenges in a gradual and intuitive way, designers can help to ensure that users feel confident and capable as they navigate through the software. By building on existing knowledge and providing clear instructions and feedback, designers can help to guide users through the onboarding process and prepare them for more advanced features and functionality later on.

Here's an example of how the double jump concept could be applied to onboarding users to a personal finance application:

Imagine a personal finance app that is designed to help users manage their money and achieve their financial goals. As part of the onboarding process, the app might ask the user what their goal is for using the app. Rather than presenting users with a long list of features and options, the app might provide three simple choices: "Save for a vacation", "Pay off debt", or "Build an emergency fund".

By presenting users with these choices, the app is providing a clear sense of direction and purpose that can help to motivate and engage users. Once the user has made their choice, the app can provide guidance and resources that are tailored to their specific goal, such as articles or tutorials on budgeting, debt reduction strategies, or emergency fund planning.

By framing the onboarding process in this way, the app is effectively creating a "high wall" that requires users to make a choice and commit to a specific goal before they can proceed. This not only helps to support the user's aspirations, but also provides a sense of progression and ownership towards their future financial success.

As users become more comfortable with the app and begin to achieve their goals, the app can introduce more advanced features and functionality, such as investment tracking, retirement planning, or tax optimization. By building on existing knowledge and providing clear feedback and guidance, the app can help users to achieve greater levels of financial mastery and control over time.

To apply the principles of the double jump concept to your own business or product onboarding process, you could create obstacles or challenges that encourage users to explore new features and capabilities. Provide clear guidance and feedback to support their learning and growth, and frame the onboarding process in a way that instills a sense of progression and ownership. By doing so, you can help to keep users engaged and motivated, leading to a more positive and successful onboarding experience.
